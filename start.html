Start

March 20, 2026

The past three years of my life have brought a period of great personal growth. After my father unexpectedly passed away in August of 2023, I left my full-time job as an investor and traveled the world for a year. I spent most of my waking hours focused on my physical and mental health, adjusting my relationship with food, drugs, alcohol, and exercise, while learning how to accept and love previously neglected parts of myself. One method of "coping" I used during this time was writing. I've been to enough casual meetings of aspiring writers to know that I'm not the first person to use my writing as a form of therapy, but it truly was a helpful way to survive the emotional whitewater I was rushing down.

The topics I wrote on were matters of my own heart and so my writing was largely a private exercise. However, I now see the importance of sharing some of my writing. I have a few reasons:

  1. A public reflecting pool: publication of work (whether printed in a national newspaper or scribbled on a piece of cardboard and tacked to the local notice board) facilitates higher quality and a greater sense of ownership. Published work is putting a stake in the ground to represent a point of view, and others can then comment on where the stake is, and later versions of myself can revisit the stake and reflect on how I might place it differently in the future. While the latter can also be done with a private journal, the effect is lessened.
  2. A foot in two worlds: from some combination of hard work and a sprinkle of luck, I've had the opportunity to work on some incredible projects (advising on investments in large public companies, and even the acquisition of the Boston Celtics) while creating a comfortable lifestyle (I live in Taiwan, work less than 40 hours a week, and am an avid student of Chinese language and culture). I see my experience as straddling two worlds: (i) the pinnacle of today's investment environment and (ii) a small sliver of the Sinosphere. I find this combination to be an engaging topic to reflect on.
  3. Rejection of the silent majority: in the last decade of my life I have been very quiet. I had nothing to say and what I did want to share I was too scared to do so for fear of being labeled wrong. I no longer believe silence is the best course. It's important to stand for what you believe in, and I have an unshakable confidence that my beliefs stem from a deep love for every person on this planet. That is worth sharing (i) to remind people that there are strangers in the world who care about them, and (ii) with the hope that this unconditional love becomes infectious.

I'm excited to keep writing. It feels like every week I learn about something new that gets my blood rushing. I look forward to writing about those topics, to see how far my curious mind will take me, and eventually look back on where it has been.

Who am I: I usually introduce myself as Andreas, but many Americans aren't familiar with this name and end up thinking I said Elias. So I often say Dre, which is what my family and friends call me, but then people always joke that I'm not much like Dr. Dre, which is of course true. The only peoples who accept my name immediately are Central Europeans and South Americans, as the name Andreas has many spellings across those regions (Andreas, Andres, Andries, Andrzej).

In Taiwan, I never introduce myself as Andreas. Any name with more than two syllables leave the Taiwanese tongue-twisted. So normally I go by 小安 (Little An), because 小 (little) is a common prefix for Taiwanese nicknames. Subsequently, I often am told this is name for women.

So in the end I will sign my work with any of the above monikers, or perhaps none at all. I don't want any one name to garner any particular reputation. Names are irrelevant but ideas are important. People and their opinions are fluid. The Dre I was ten years ago is not the 小安 I am today, so even if the name on the page is still my own the idea might not be.
